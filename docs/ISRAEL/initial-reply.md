It's not just about civil liberties broadly, but specifically about the *relationship between religion and state*—and whether a government uses religious doctrine to justify oppression, violence against its own people, or the denial of basic freedoms like speech, conscience, or equality before the law.

On that axis, you're right to see a vast difference between Israel and Iran—even if both have religious elements in their identity.

Israel defines itself as a Jewish state, but its legal system is secular in practice: civil courts handle most matters, religious minorities (Muslim, Christian, Druze, Bahá'í, etc.) have legal recognition and communal autonomy, and while Jewish personal status law (marriage, divorce) is controlled by rabbinic authorities—a point of deep controversy—there is no death penalty for apostasy, no legal enforcement of religious dress or prayer, and Arab Israelis vote, serve in parliament, and sit on the Supreme Court. Yes, there is systemic discrimination against Arab citizens in funding, policing, and national narrative—but the framework allows for legal challenge, protest, and change. That matters.

Iran, by contrast, is an Islamic Republic where sovereignty is held to derive from divine law as interpreted by Shi'a clerics. The constitution requires all laws to conform to Islam; the Guardian Council (half clerical) vetoes legislation and screens candidates; apostasy *can* be punished by death (though rarely enacted); Baha'is are persecuted as heretics; women face legal inequities in testimony, inheritance, and dress; and the state actively promotes jihadist ideology abroad.

So yes: on separation of religion and state, protection of religious minorities, and freedom from religious coercion, Israel is *qualitatively* closer to liberal democracies like the U.S. (despite its Christian cultural majority) than to Iran or Saudi Arabia or Taliban Afghanistan.

But—and this is where it gets hard—you have to reckon with how that internal liberalism coexists with military occupation, blockade, and disproportionate force in Gaza and the West Bank. You can believe Israel is the least bad option in the region *and* still condemn specific policies that kill civilians, destroy homes, or deny self-determination. The metric you’re proposing doesn’t excuse misconduct—it just says: *evaluate the regime’s direction, not just its worst acts.*

So if your goal is to support civilizations with better ideas, then the question becomes: does supporting Israel—flaws and all—strengthen or weaken the global spread of those ideas? Does it empower moderates and reformers within Israeli and Palestinian society, or does it entrench hardliners on both sides who thrive on perpetual conflict?

That’s where I’d push: Is unconditional support the best way to advance liberal ideals in Israel/Palestine? Or would conditioning aid on concrete steps toward equality, religious freedom, and an end to occupation actually serve those ideals better—by forcing the system to live up to its own claims?

You want to reward good ideas. Fair. But sometimes the best way to reward them is to make their survival *depend* on living up to them.
