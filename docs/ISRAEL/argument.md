# Thesis: America should support Israel because it is a real ally and the regional civilization closest to American ideals

**Abstract:** The strongest case for American support of Israel is not that Israel is morally spotless, nor that civilian casualties do not matter. The case is comparative and civilizational: among the realistic powers in the Middle East, Israel is far closer to the American constitutional tradition than the Islamist and authoritarian alternatives around it. It is also an actual U.S. security partner, even if it is not a NATO treaty ally. That does not require unconditional support; it requires support ordered toward the survival and improvement of the side whose institutions, ideals, and strategic alignment are closest to ours.

## Supporting Clause: "Ally" does not only mean NATO treaty ally

### Definitions and supporting evidence

`Ally:` In this argument, an ally is a state whose long-term strategic cooperation, intelligence sharing, military coordination, and institutional alignment materially advance American interests. A formal mutual-defense treaty is one kind of alliance, but not the only meaningful kind.

`Treaty ally:` A state to which the United States has a formal mutual-defense commitment, such as NATO allies. Israel is not a NATO treaty ally, so if "ally" means only this narrow legal category, the interlocutor is correct.

`Major non-NATO ally:` A legal designation under U.S. law that gives selected partners defense trade and security cooperation benefits without creating a mutual-defense guarantee.

> "Major non-NATO ally (MNNA) status is a designation under U.S. law that provides foreign partners with certain benefits in the areas of defense trade and security cooperation. While MNNA status provides military and economic privileges, it does not entail any security commitments to the designated country."

**Source:** U.S. Department of State. *Major Non-NATO Ally Status*. https://2017-2021.state.gov/major-non-nato-ally-status

> "Israel was designated as a major non-NATO ally in 1987."

**Source:** U.S. Department of State. *U.S. Relations With Israel*. https://www.state.gov/u-s-relations-with-israel-

> "The United States and Israel signed a 10-year Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) on military aid in 2016, covering FY2019-FY2028. Under the MOU, the United States pledged to provide Israel $38 billion in military aid."

**Source:** Congressional Research Service. *U.S. Foreign Aid to Israel: Overview and Developments since October 7, 2023*. https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/RL33222

### Narrative

The claim "Israel is not an ally" works only by narrowing the word ally until it means "formal treaty ally." That is a useful distinction, but it does not settle the political question. The United States has many important partners that are not NATO members, and Israel is not merely a country we "do business with." It is formally recognized as a major non-NATO ally, receives long-term security assistance through negotiated memoranda, and cooperates with the United States on intelligence, defense technology, missile defense, and regional deterrence.

So the better formulation is: Israel is not a treaty ally like Britain or Canada, but it is an American ally in the ordinary strategic sense. The absence of a NATO-style Article 5 obligation does not make Israel equivalent to Ethiopia or any other ordinary trading partner.

## Supporting Clause: Civilian deaths are morally serious, but they are not the whole moral comparison

### Definitions and supporting evidence

`Civilian death standard:` The moral principle that civilian deaths count heavily against a state, especially when they result from recklessness, cruelty, or indifference.

`Regime comparison:` A broader moral and political evaluation of what a state is, what it upholds, how it treats dissent, whether its institutions can self-correct, and what kind of civilization its victory would strengthen.

`Reversibility:` The same principle should apply if actors are swapped. If civilian death is the sole standard, then every state that kills civilians in war becomes morally equivalent. If regime character matters, then civilian death remains relevant without erasing all distinctions between regimes.

### Narrative

The interlocutor's strongest point is that mass civilian death should not be shrugged off because the state causing it is "our ally." That point is right. A pro-Israel argument that treats Palestinian civilian life as morally weightless is not worth defending.

But it does not follow that civilian deaths are the only relevant moral variable. War can make very different regimes look similar if the only metric is "innocents died." The United States, Britain, Israel, Iran, Russia, Hamas, and ISIS have all been connected to civilian deaths in different ways. That fact matters, but it does not make their political orders morally identical.

The real question is not "has this side ever done something terrible?" The real question is "what kind of society does this side build, what kind of order does it defend, and what happens if its enemies win?" On that axis, Israel is closer to the American side of the ledger than to the Islamist or authoritarian alternatives.

## Supporting Clause: Israel is closer to American ideals than the Islamist Middle East

### Definitions and supporting evidence

`American ideals:` Constitutional government, representative politics, freedom of speech and conscience, equality before the law, religious pluralism, independent courts, and the possibility of peaceful reform through institutions.

`Islamist Middle East:` In this argument, not Muslims as people and not every Muslim-majority country, but political movements and regimes that subordinate civil government to religious doctrine and use state or militia power to enforce that doctrine.

`Civilizational alignment:` The degree to which a state upholds, imperfectly but materially, the same institutional and moral principles the United States should want to see survive.

> "Israel is a parliamentary democracy with a multiparty system and independent institutions that generally guarantee political rights and civil liberties for the population within its borders."

**Source:** Freedom House. *Israel: Freedom in the World 2025*. https://freedomhouse.org/country/israel/freedom-world/2025

> Freedom House rates Israel "Free" while noting serious concerns, including pressure on judicial independence and "long-term discrimination against Arab and other ethnic and religious minority populations."

**Source:** Freedom House. *Israel: Freedom in the World 2025*. https://freedomhouse.org/country/israel/freedom-world/2025

> Arab citizens of Israel vote in Knesset elections, Arab-led parties compete for seats, and Israeli courts have sometimes protected Arab parties' right to run.

**Source:** Israel Democracy Institute. *An Elections for the 25th Knesset: An Analysis of the Results in the Arab Sector*. https://en.idi.org.il/articles/47986; Adalah. *Israel's top court unanimously overturns ban on Arab party from running*. https://www.adalah.org/en/content/view/10705

### Narrative

Israel is not America. It has religious establishment problems, ethnic tensions, security-state excesses, unequal treatment of Arab citizens, and the unresolved moral disaster of occupation and war. But its political form is still recognizably closer to the American model than Iran, Hamas, Hezbollah, the Houthis, Saudi Arabia, Taliban Afghanistan, or the jihadist movements that dominate much of the regional anti-Israel coalition.

Israel has elections, parties, courts, protest movements, opposition media, religious minorities, gay citizens living openly in ways impossible in much of the region, and Arab citizens who vote and serve in the legislature. Those facts do not erase discrimination or Palestinian suffering. They do show that Israel contains mechanisms of self-criticism and reform that its enemies generally lack.

This is the civilizational point: American support should favor societies whose institutions can absorb criticism, protect minorities imperfectly but meaningfully, and move closer to liberal norms over time. If the choice is not between Israel and utopia, but between Israel and the forces that would replace it, Israel is the side closer to American ideals.

## Argument: Support Israel, but support the Israel that lives up to its own best principles

### Definitions and supporting evidence

`Support:` Political, diplomatic, military, and moral backing sufficient to preserve Israel's security and regional deterrence. Support does not mean endorsing every Israeli policy, every Israeli government, or every action in Gaza or the West Bank.

`Unconditional support:` Backing that continues regardless of conduct. This argument does not require unconditional support.

`America-first support:` Support that asks whether backing Israel advances American security, American ideals, and a regional order less hostile to liberal civilization.

### Narrative

America should support Israel because Israel is a real ally and because, among the actual powers in the region, it is far closer to the civilization America should want strengthened. That is the core argument. It is not a claim that every Israeli action is justified, or that Palestinian civilian deaths are irrelevant, or that American policy should be written by pro-Israel donors or foreign interests.

The pro-Israel case becomes strongest when it is disciplined by American interests and American ideals. Israel should be supported as a partner against jihadist and Iranian-backed power, as a democratic outpost in a hostile region, and as a society with enough liberal structure to be criticized, pressured, and improved. But the support should aim at the survival of that better Israel: one that protects its citizens, resists annihilationist enemies, and also restrains itself where possible because civilian life and equal dignity are part of the civilization it claims to defend.

The answer to "America first or Israel first?" should be: America first, and therefore support Israel where Israel serves American security and civilizational interests. If Israel becomes merely another ethnonationalist or religious state indifferent to liberal norms, the case for support weakens. But as things stand, Israel's enemies are not offering a more American, more liberal, or more humane order. They are offering a worse one.

## Refutation to: Support Israel | Argument: "Israel is not really an ally"

### Definitions and supporting evidence

> "In what way is Israel an ally? They're not even an ally as technically defined by the US. It's just a talking point. England is an ally. Canada is an ally. Israel is not. It's a country. We do business with sure. Just like Ethiopia."

**Source:** `initial-position.md`

### Narrative

This objection is strongest when it distinguishes formal treaty allies from looser strategic partners. Britain and Canada are treaty allies in a way Israel is not. The United States has no automatic Article 5-style obligation to defend Israel.

But the objection overreaches when it concludes that Israel is therefore not an ally at all. U.S. law and policy recognize categories of security partnership outside NATO. Israel's major non-NATO ally status, long-term aid memoranda, missile-defense cooperation, intelligence relationship, and regional deterrent role make it a real strategic ally. The proper correction is precision, not dismissal: Israel is not a treaty ally, but it is a major non-NATO ally and strategic partner.

## Refutation to: Support Israel | Argument: "Israel has not fought wars on America's behalf"

### Definitions and supporting evidence

> "How many wars has Israel fought on our behalf? Answer zero."

**Source:** `initial-position.md`

### Narrative

This objection assumes that an ally proves its value mainly by fighting America's wars. That is too narrow. Some allies contribute by hosting bases, some by sharing intelligence, some by deterring regional enemies, some by developing military technology, and some by absorbing threats that would otherwise strengthen hostile blocs.

It is fair to ask whether Israel's actions ever drag America toward unnecessary conflict. That is a real strategic concern. But "has not fought our wars" does not prove "not an ally." The question is whether Israel's existence and partnership improve America's strategic position compared with a Middle East dominated by Iranian proxies, jihadist movements, or anti-American authoritarian coalitions.

## Refutation to: Support Israel | Argument: "Civilian slaughter makes pro-Israel politics morally incoherent"

### Definitions and supporting evidence

> "People are upset with mass civilian slaughter. That totally makes sense. Anyone that doesn't care about that doesn't have the requisite empathy to be of sound mind."

**Source:** `initial-position.md`

> "These positions are often cited and contradict each other: 'The Iranian regime is so repressive to the Iranian population' ... and 'Why do Americans care so much about Palestinian civilians being murdered in large numbers by Israel?' These contradict as both sides kill innocent civilians."

**Source:** `initial-position.md`

### Narrative

The steelman is that Israel's defenders sometimes use "ally" as a moral solvent: civilian deaths become tolerable because the actor causing them is on our side. That is wrong. Civilian life is not made cheap by alliance.

But the proposed contradiction dissolves once we distinguish regime character from wartime conduct. Iran's regime can be repressive because of how it is constituted, how it treats its citizens, how it subordinates law to clerical rule, and how it exports violent ideology. Israel can commit grave wrongs in war without becoming morally identical to Iran. Conversely, condemning Israel's conduct in Gaza does not require pretending Hamas, Iran, or Hezbollah represent an equal or better civilizational alternative.

The consistent position is: oppose civilian slaughter, oppose Islamist authoritarianism, support the side whose institutions are closer to liberal democracy, and pressure that side harder precisely because it claims to share our values.

## Refutation to: Support Israel | Argument: "Pro-Israel influence corrupts America-first politics"

### Definitions and supporting evidence

> "Israel just spent a lot of money in an electoral race in Kentucky. A pro Israel candidate just won thru their influence."

**Source:** `initial-position.md`

> "The Israel-first people in the US and inside of Israel, have far more power and influence than is good for America. Trump should be America-first, but he can tend to be Israel-first."

**Source:** `initial-position.md`

### Narrative

This objection should not be dismissed. Foreign-policy lobbies, donor networks, and ideological factions can distort American judgment. A serious pro-Israel position must reject the idea that loyalty to Israel outranks loyalty to the United States.

The answer is not to deny influence exists. The answer is to put the alliance inside an America-first framework. Israel deserves support only where that support advances American security, American ideals, and a better regional order. If a policy serves Israel but damages America, America should reject it. If a donor network punishes legitimate criticism of Israeli policy, Americans should resist that too.

But corrupt influence is not the same question as whether Israel is worth supporting. The existence of a bad lobby argument does not defeat a good civilizational and strategic argument.

## Open questions

- What concrete conditions, if any, should America attach to military aid so support strengthens Israel's liberal-democratic character rather than shielding its worst policies?
- What evidence best measures Israel's net strategic value to the United States: intelligence cooperation, technology transfer, deterrence, regional escalation risk, domestic political cost, or something else?
- How should the argument weigh Palestinians who are neither Hamas nor Islamist and who also aspire to dignity, self-government, and freedom from religious authoritarianism?
- What is the limiting principle for "civilizational support" so it does not excuse atrocities by friendly states?
