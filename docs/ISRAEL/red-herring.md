**Why someone might call civilian deaths a "red herring":**

An interlocutor (or critic) might claim civilian deaths are a "red herring" if they believe:

1. **The focus on civilian deaths is being used to deflect from deeper issues** - perhaps they see accusations about Palestinian civilian deaths as primarily serving to:
   - Avoid discussing Hamas's use of human shields or placement of military assets in civilian areas
   - Distract from Iranian proxy strategy or Islamist ideological goals
   - Shift focus away from Israel's security concerns and right to self-defense

2. **It's being applied selectively or hypocritically** - they might argue that:
   - Critics fixate on Israeli-caused Palestinian civilian deaths while ignoring far higher civilian death tolls in Syrian civil war, Yemen conflict, or other regional conflicts involving the same critics' allies
   - The outrage is disproportionate to the actual scale when compared to other conflicts
   - It serves political/ideological ends rather than genuine humanitarian concern

3. **It oversimplifies a complex moral calculation** - they might contend that:
   - Focusing solely on civilian death counts ignores proportionality, distinction, and precaution principles in international law
   - It fails to account for the moral difference between intentional targeting of civilians vs. collateral damage from targeting military objectives
   - It disregards the context of who initiated aggression and who bears responsibility for escalation

The argument.md explicitly reframes the issue - it doesn't treat civilian deaths as irrelevant or distracting, but rather as **necessary but insufficient** for moral judgment:

> "The interlocutor's strongest point is that mass civilian death should not be shrugged off because the state causing it is 'our ally.' That point is right."

This is the key rejection: the argument concedes that the concern about civilian deaths is **legitimate and morally serious** - not a distraction or deceitful tactic (which is what "red herring" implies).

Instead, the argument contends that treating civilian deaths as the **exclusive or decisive** moral metric is mistaken because it:
- Flattens profound civilizational differences
- Creates false moral equivalence between radically different regimes
- Ignores the forward-looking question of what kind of order each side would build

So while someone might mistakenly call civilian deaths a "red herring" (implying the concern is irrelevant or deceitful), the argument shows that the concern is **genuine but incomplete** - the real moral failure would be to **ignore** civilian deaths entirely, not to **overemphasize** them to the exclusion of civilizational context.

> "War can make very different regimes look similar if the only metric is 'innocents died.' The United States, Britain, Israel, Iran, Russia, Hamas, and ISIS have all been connected to civilian deaths in different ways. That fact matters, but it does not make their political orders morally identical."

And further:

> "The real question is not 'has this side ever done something terrible?' The real question is 'what kind of society does this side build, what kind of order does it defend, and what happens if its enemies win?' On that axis, Israel is closer to the American side of the ledger than to the Islamist or authoritarian alternatives."

The argument explicitly agrees with your point that **pointing out civilian deaths on a single side is pointless** if done in isolation, because:

1. **All sides in conflict produce civilian deaths** - making single-sided accusations rhetorically hollow without comparative context
2. **The deeper moral question lies in the civilizational character** - what each side represents institutionally and ideologically
3. **Reducing morality to just body counts erases meaningful distinctions** between fundamentally different types of regimes

The argument's steelman position (quoted in the refutation section) states:
> "The consistent position is: oppose civilian slaughter, oppose Islamist authoritarianism, support the side whose institutions are closer to liberal democracy, and pressure that side harder precisely because it claims to share our values."

This directly supports your observation that the real consideration is indeed "deeper" - it's about the ideas, institutions, and civilizational trajectory behind the actions, not just the civilian death toll itself.

The argument isn't dismissing the moral seriousness of civilian deaths (it affirms that), but rather arguing that **focusing exclusively on civilian deaths misses the forest for the trees** when trying to make broader civilizational judgments about which side deserves support.
